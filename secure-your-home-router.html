Let's Make Your Wifi Router as Secure as Possible

Router manufacturers are simplifying setup and configuration processes, often through user-friendly apps. Despite these advancements, most people only perform basic setup tasks such as renaming their WiFi and changing passwords, neglecting further customization. This minimal approach may work for immediate connectivity needs and might seem secure enough to deter potential hackers. However, there's more that can be done to enhance your router's security and the overall safety of your home network.

It's important to note that wireless routers vary significantly in their interfaces, terminology, and available settings. For instance, when navigating the settings of a TP-Link Archer C7 for this discussion, keep in mind that your router's configuration options and how you access them may differ. You should familiarize yourself with your router’s configuration interface—be it web-based or app-driven—to ensure you're maximizing security settings. Be aware, however, that you might not be able to implement every security measure suggested here due to these differences.

Accessing your router's settings

If your router lacks a straightforward app for setup, you typically configure its settings by launching a web browser on a device connected to the router and entering the router's IP address.

  • On a Windows system, pull up the command prompt and type in ipconfig. The IP address that's listed as your default gateway is likely your router's IP address.
  • If you're on a Mac, pull up System Preferences > Network, click on Advanced in the bottom-right corner, then click on the TCP/IP option toward the top of the next window and look for your router's IP address.
  • If you're on your iPhone, tap on Settings, then Wi-Fi, and tap on the “i” icon next to the wifi network you're connected to. Your router's IP address should be listed right there.

Step One: Update your firmware

Some routers keep firmware updates deep inside their settings menus; others may even inform you of a new firmware update right away when you go into their applications or web-based user interfaces. Whatever option you select, you should ensure that your router has the latest firmware.

Change your router login and password

If you're still using “admin / admin,” “admin / password,” or some variant of generic words to log into your router, change that. Even if your router manufacturer has given you a quirkier password that presumably differs for everybody, it's important to use a login and password that's tough to guess or brute-force. Even if you're stuck using “admin” as a user name to log in, make your password something complex, not something anyone can look up via a quick web search.

Use WPA3 to secure your wireless network

To improve the security of your wireless network, utilise WPA3, the most recent Wi-Fi Protected Access protocol. WPA3 improves on previous versions, delivering improved data protection for your network. Here are the reasons to choose WPA3:

  • Enhanced Security: WPA3 offers stronger encryption through the Simultaneous Authentication of Equals (SAE) protocol, which significantly improves protection against offline dictionary attacks. This means even if an attacker captures data from your network, deciphering it without the exact password becomes much more difficult.
  • Increased Protection for Public Networks: WPA3 also introduces individualized data encryption on open networks. This means that each user's connection is encrypted separately, enhancing privacy and security even on networks that don't require a password.
  • Better Device Protection: Devices that support WPA3 are required to use stronger encryption methods, providing additional protection against certain types of attacks that could compromise your device.

If your devices do not support WPA3, it's crucial to ensure you're using a strong, complex password for your Wi-Fi network. A strong password can mitigate many of the vulnerabilities that older security protocols have, making it significantly harder for attackers to gain unauthorized access to your network.
